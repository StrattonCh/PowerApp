<h2>Distribution: Normal </h2>

<h3>Statistic: $\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$</h3>

<h4>Alternative: Greater than</h4>

Suppose that $X_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$, where $\sigma^2$ is known, and we take a random sample of size $n$ from this population. Further suppose that we wish to test the following hypotheses:
\[
\begin{split}
\text{$H_0$: }& \theta \leq \theta_0 \\
\text{$H_a$: }& \theta > \theta_0
\end{split}
\]

Consider the following test function:
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i > k \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases},
\]

where $k$ is chosen such that $Pr(\phi(X) = 1|\theta_0) = \alpha$. It can be shown that if $X_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$, then $\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \sim \text{N}(n\theta, n\sigma^2)$. Using this relationship, we derive the test. 
\[
\begin{split}
Pr(\phi(X) = 1|\theta_0) = Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i > k\Big|\theta_0\right) = \alpha 
\end{split}
\]

Let $T = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \sim \text{N}(n\theta, n\sigma^2)$. To define our test, we seek the value $k$ such that 
\[
Pr(T > k | \theta_0) = 1 - Pr(T \leq k | \theta_0) = \alpha
\]

The value of $k$ that satisfies this equation is the $(1 - \alpha)^{th}$ quantile of the $\text{N}(n\theta_0, n\sigma^2)$ distribution. Letting $z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha}$ denote this value, our test functions becomes
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i > z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha} \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases}
\]

Therefore, our power function is
\[
\beta(\theta) = Pr(X \in RR) = Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i > z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha} \Big| \theta\right) = 1 - Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \leq z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha}  \Big| \theta\right)
\]

<h4>Alternative: Less than</h4>

Suppose that $X_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$, where $\sigma^2$ is known, and we take a random sample of size $n$ from this population. Further suppose that we wish to test the following hypotheses:
\[
\begin{split}
\text{$H_0$: }& \theta \geq \theta_0 \\
\text{$H_a$: }& \theta < \theta_0
\end{split}
\]

Consider the following test function:
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i < k \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases},
\]

where $k$ is chosen such that $Pr(\phi(X) = 1|\theta_0) = \alpha$. Let $T = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i \sim \text{N}(n\theta, n\sigma^2)$. To define our test, we seek the value $k$ such that 
\[
Pr(T < k | \theta_0) = \alpha
\]

The value of $k$ that satisfies this equation is the $\alpha^{th}$ quantile of the $\text{N}(n\theta_0, n\sigma^2)$ distribution. Letting $z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, \alpha}$ denote this value, our test function becomes
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i < z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, \alpha} \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases}
\]

Therefore, our power function is
\[
\beta(\theta) = Pr(X \in RR) = Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i < z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, \alpha} \Big| \theta\right) 
\]

<h4>Alternative: Not equal to</h4>

Suppose that $X_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{N}(\theta, \sigma^2)$, where $\sigma^2$ is known, and we take a random sample of size $n$ from this population. Further suppose that we wish to test the following hypotheses:
\[
\begin{split}
\text{$H_0$: }& \theta = \theta_0 \\
\text{$H_a$: }& \theta \neq \theta_0
\end{split}
\]

Consider the following test function:
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i > |k| \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases},
\]

where $k$ are is chosen such that $Pr(\phi(X) = 1|\theta_0) = \alpha$. Note that we assume a symmetric test function in that $k$ is chosen such that $Pr(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i < -k) = \alpha/2$ and $Pr(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i > k) = \alpha/2$. Let $T = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim \text{N}(n\theta, n\sigma^2)$. The value of $k$ that satisfies this equation is the $(1-\alpha/2)^{th}$ quantile of the $\text{N}{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2}$ distribution. Letting $z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha/2}$ denote this value, our test function becomes
\[
\phi(X) = \begin{cases}
1 & \sum_{i=1}^n X_i < z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, \alpha/2} \text{ or } \sum_{i=1}^n X_i > z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha/2} \\
0 & \text{else}
\end{cases}
\]

Therefore, our power function is
\[
\beta(\theta) = Pr(X \in RR) = 1 - Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i < z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, 1-\alpha/2}\right) +  Pr\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i < z^*_{n\theta_0, n\sigma^2, \alpha/2} \Big| \theta\right) 
\]
